# Assessment Argument

## Purpose

This framework supports scientifically defensible interpretation of pre-service teachers' Decision Tree understanding and provides a traceable pathway to provisional UNESCO AI-CFT competency claims.

## Central Argument

If:

- raw learner evidence is captured from multiple relevant sources,
- evidence is converted into traceable evidence units,
- evidence units are interpreted as observable behaviours,
- behaviours are mapped to instructional Learning Objects,
- Learning Objects are aggregated into coherent Domain Understanding,
- Domain Understanding is interpreted through AI-CFT only after sufficient evidence has accumulated,
- and researchers review the full evidence trail,

then:

- the resulting interpretation is more valid, explainable, and defensible than direct worksheet scoring or direct worksheet-to-competency mapping.

## Layered Assessment Argument

### Layer 1 Claim: Raw Evidence Is Adequately Captured

Claim:

- the available source artifacts are sufficient to support later evidence extraction

Evidence:

- worksheets
- CODAP logs
- screen recordings
- reflections
- observations
- interviews when available

Warrant:

- valid interpretation depends on preserving source-native evidence before transformation

Backing:

- ECD requires explicit linkage between claims and evidentiary foundations

Alternative explanations:

- a missing or partial source may distort the learner profile

Counter-evidence:

- absent files, corrupted recordings, incomplete logs, illegible worksheet regions

Evidence sufficiency:

- enough source material must exist to support at least one interpretable evidence stream

Confidence requirements:

- provenance and completeness of source capture must be documented

Threats to validity:

- source loss
- source imbalance
- preprocessing damage

Mitigation strategies:

- source manifests
- missing-data flags
- source quality indicators

### Layer 2 Claim: Evidence Units Faithfully Represent Source Data

Claim:

- extracted evidence units accurately represent interpretable portions of the raw evidence

Evidence:

- source-linked excerpts, event records, timestamped episodes, paragraph spans

Warrant:

- higher-level interpretation is only defensible if extraction preserves meaning and provenance

Backing:

- learning analytics and multimodal assessment depend on atomic, traceable records

Alternative explanations:

- extraction artifacts may reflect OCR error, segmentation error, or transcription loss

Counter-evidence:

- mismatches between source and extracted content

Evidence sufficiency:

- each unit must include source, location, content, confidence, provenance, uncertainty, and alternative interpretations

Confidence requirements:

- extraction confidence must be explicit and separable from interpretation confidence

Threats to validity:

- OCR noise
- timestamp misalignment
- ambiguous segmentation

Mitigation strategies:

- source-specific extraction schemas
- low-confidence review flags
- preserved links to original source regions

### Layer 3 Claim: Observable Behaviours Are Defensibly Inferred

Claim:

- the evidence supports a specific observable learner behaviour

Evidence:

- one or more evidence units showing an action, explanation, revision, comparison, or judgement

Warrant:

- behaviour is the first defensible interpretive layer above raw evidence

Backing:

- ECD supports inference from observations to structured behavioural indicators

Alternative explanations:

- the learner may have guessed, copied, or produced the behaviour superficially

Counter-evidence:

- contradictory behaviours in other sources
- incomplete or inconsistent reasoning

Evidence sufficiency:

- behaviour claims require direct evidence and should not be inferred from task completion alone

Confidence requirements:

- behaviour-level confidence must consider extraction quality, evidence clarity, and ambiguity

Threats to validity:

- activity mistaken for understanding
- frequency mistaken for strategy
- fluent language mistaken for sound reasoning

Mitigation strategies:

- explicit behaviour definitions
- misconception links
- evidence strength ceilings

### Layer 4 Claim: Learning Objects Are Supported

Claim:

- observable behaviours support a targeted instructional concept or skill

Evidence:

- convergent behaviours aligned with one or more Learning Objects

Warrant:

- Learning Objects represent the intended instructional meaning of behaviours

Backing:

- instructional targets provide finer-grained interpretation than broad competency labels

Alternative explanations:

- a behaviour may reflect partial, shallow, or misconceived knowledge

Counter-evidence:

- behaviours linked to related misconceptions

Evidence sufficiency:

- Learning Object support should rely on behaviour patterns rather than isolated correctness where possible

Confidence requirements:

- confidence must reflect consistency across behaviours and source appropriateness

Threats to validity:

- conflating terminology recall with conceptual understanding
- conflating procedure following with conceptual mastery

Mitigation strategies:

- behaviour-to-LO mapping rules
- misconception ontology
- source-diversity checks

### Layer 5 Claim: Domain Understanding Is Demonstrated

Claim:

- the learner demonstrates a broader form of Decision Tree understanding

Evidence:

- coordinated support across multiple Learning Objects and behaviours

Warrant:

- disciplinary understanding is broader than isolated concepts and should emerge from patterns of evidence

Backing:

- competency-based assessment requires synthesis beyond individual items

Alternative explanations:

- isolated strengths may mask fragmented understanding

Counter-evidence:

- contradictory Learning Object patterns
- missing transfer evidence
- repeated misconceptions

Evidence sufficiency:

- broader domain claims should require converging evidence across tasks, sources, or occasions whenever appropriate

Confidence requirements:

- confidence must reflect breadth, coherence, contradiction, and source coverage

Threats to validity:

- construct underrepresentation
- overgeneralization from a single task family

Mitigation strategies:

- multidimensional Domain Understanding model
- contradiction tracking
- sufficiency thresholds

### Layer 6 Claim: AI-CFT Interpretation Is Provisionally Justified

Claim:

- the accumulated Domain Understanding supports a provisional AI-CFT competency claim

Evidence:

- explicit domain-level evidence trail with rationale and uncertainty

Warrant:

- UNESCO AI-CFT is a higher-order interpretive framework and should be grounded in domain evidence rather than worksheet performance

Backing:

- competency interpretation is stronger when disciplinary evidence is already assembled and scrutinized

Alternative explanations:

- an AI-CFT claim may reflect local task success rather than broader capability

Counter-evidence:

- weak domain breadth
- unresolved contradictions
- sparse evidence

Evidence sufficiency:

- AI-CFT claims must never rest on a single worksheet, a single behaviour, or a direct LO-to-competency shortcut

Confidence requirements:

- AI-CFT confidence must be lower than or equal to the underlying domain evidence confidence

Threats to validity:

- premature competency assignment
- framework overreach

Mitigation strategies:

- keep claims provisional
- require researcher review
- preserve explicit mapping logic

### Layer 7 Claim: Final Judgement Is Human-Validated

Claim:

- the final reported competency interpretation is researcher-owned and auditably justified

Evidence:

- supporting evidence
- contradictory evidence
- confidence
- uncertainty
- traceability
- alternative explanations
- researcher rationale

Warrant:

- final competency decisions require human accountability in high-stakes interpretive contexts

Backing:

- human-in-the-loop assessment reduces over-automation and improves transparency

Alternative explanations:

- the machine recommendation may still overstate or understate competence

Counter-evidence:

- researcher disagreement with the proposed interpretation

Evidence sufficiency:

- a final decision should not be made without inspectable evidence and explicit rationale

Confidence requirements:

- final reporting must distinguish machine confidence from researcher judgement

Threats to validity:

- automation bias
- reviewer inconsistency

Mitigation strategies:

- researcher review protocol
- reviewer training and calibration
- decision audit trail

## Global Warrants

- multiple evidence sources represent the construct better than worksheets alone
- observable behaviour is a stronger inferential basis than raw score
- Learning Objects are needed to preserve instructional meaning
- Domain Understanding is the proper primary assessment layer
- AI-CFT should remain a final interpretive layer

## Interpretation Rule

No single worksheet, item, or source may determine AI-CFT interpretation on its own.

The framework supports provisional recommendations only. Final competency assignment remains a human research decision.
